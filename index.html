Friends of the Internet, Where do I belong?

Making Internet has become the truest, and most vital, of the humanities!

The Internet is the most interesting thing...maybe ever. It's getting exponentially more interesting as it swallows or disrupts everything from information and biology, to the planet and even the cosmos! Network culture and software are the legos we're using to build this slice of the near-future with, and right now both pursuits badly need more design thinking to help identify and solve the right problems. Designers must broker the relationship between humans and computers well enough to get all of the humans not only using the network, but also making it for themselves. It is thrilling because it has so much potential for both good and evil. It demands my full attention.

So, friends, please help me find where I belong. Choosing what to work on, and who to spend the majority of your waking hours with is never easy, and I tend to really dig into a project and see it all the way through, so it's not a light decision. I made this site to paint a more complete picture of who I am, and what I'm looking for, than my resume and portfolio can. If I could write my own ticket, it'd look something like this:

I am seeking a lead design role on a life-changing, internet software project. With any luck, one of the lives it's been changing (or will) is mine. Ideally this role exists on a small, cross-functional, highly-autonomous team of self-managed, chummy, curious, achievers, who move swiftly to gather and measure user feedback and behavior, which they use to plan, build, and ship interesting and useful products and features. Even more ideally, this team lives within a optimistic and cheerful, socially and environmentally responsible company or organization, with a strong long-term vision and sense of values, who's users' success and happiness are directly linked to it's subsistence.

Please keep scrolling to read a bit more about me, have a look at my work, and my resume to get a sense of what I can bring to the table. Then, please say hello!

In case you're not into that stuff, here's a story instead:

Luckily, I did everything wrong.

Thirteen years ago, I dropped out of art school in Colorado, to live the rap-song cliché of making it big on the internet in New York City. What seemed (even to me) like a slew of dubious decisions at the time, could not have prepared me more perfectly for the way the world has been turning out. Young and hungry, I had motive and opportunity to become skilled and practiced among the best and brightest.

In those early years nothing on the internet really worked yet. It was supposed to change the world, but no one really knew how. The browsers were warring, connection speeds were slow, and platforms were numerous and treacherous. In those strange days after the first bust, before the next boom, while Apple was just starting to regain it's foothold, most of the money for "design" came from advertisers, so I became experienced at designing flashy projects in war-time conditions, first at a boutique studio, and later as a contractor for big-time ad agencies. Shortly after the critical mass hit (in my estimation somewhere around 2007 at the "early majority" stage of Facebook) I transitioned totally from client work to web ui/product design.

Skipping ahead a few years to today, we're in the age of "mobile" and the browsers, platforms, and connection speeds to support have multiplied! It all turned out to be good practice for my latest (and greatest) project: a complete re-design of TheLadders core product. In six months we built a brand-new application, and since I was involved from very beginning, and my awesome team was eager to experiment, we test-drove a number of new approaches including responsive design, an atomic-style organization of components with a sass pattern library, and a new workflow I designed for collaborating on design and copy assets in version control. Along the way there were some lulz and cats.

If Ray Kurzweil is right, and we are going to live forever, (and I keep up my lucky-streak), I'd love to learn new skills like data visualization, 3D, and motion graphics, explore generative art, metalworking, study philosophy, science and the cosmos, and visit the whole world. Mostly, I hope future versions of this story include many more tales of interesting projects, from the Internet Age and beyond, that have an important part to play in making our future one of the good ones.
